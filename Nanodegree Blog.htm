Google Play Store Apps – How will you succeed?

A data-based approach to understanding Kaggle’s Google Play Store dataset

 

Introduction:

 

Google’s Play App Store hosts one of the world’s largest collection of mobile apps with a reported 2.6 million as of December 2018. With many developers designing, creating and uploading apps every day, how do they differentiate themselves in this seemingly endless sea of Apps?

Due to this I decided to analyse Google Play store data, as it was available within Kaggle, to see if there any methods of differentiating your app to give it the best chance of success within the market and to potentially inform developers where to focus their app development efforts.

With this in mind, the three main areas I analysed were:

1.       How do paid vs free apps differentiate in number of installs?

2.       How do paid vs free apps differentiate in app rating?

3.       How does the app category potentially impact the rating the app receives?

 

First Analysis – Installs for Free vs Paid Apps

 

I approach the first analysis of the free vs paid app by number of installs by looking at my data and looking at the summary statistics of the installs column. The summary statistics also showed that the mean of the free apps was 19,225,817 (with a standard deviation of 94,444,993) and the mean of the paid apps was 113,010 (with a standard deviation of 704, 121).

This initial analysis suggested that a logarithmic transformation of the data would be applicable to ensure that the range of value was more easily analysed when plotted, as shown in the plot below.

 

This analysis shows that free apps, while having a significantly larger variance in values, also have on average a significantly higher installs base, not a massive surprise but it’s interesting to see how large the disparity is between these two business models. Let’s move onto the second analysis to see how rating is affected in the free vs paid app models.

 

Second Analysis – Ratings for Free vs Paid Apps

 

For the second analysis, I looked at how rating was affected by the free vs paid app business model.

Summary statistics have the free app ratings mean at 4.18 and the paid at 4.27, with standard deviations of .51 and .54 respectively. This is demonstrated in the plot below.

 

As you can see form this plot, the rating value for paid apps is slightly higher than the free apps. Meaning that users seem to find the paid apps more useful, which is an interesting consideration for developers and business thinking of charging for their apps.

Conclusion

 

The first two analyses suggest a very interesting dynamic for free vs paid apps in the app store for the number of installs and rating that the apps receive. The data shows that free apps have a significantly higher install base (and much higher variance) while paid apps remain relatively low.

Conversely, the opposite is true for ratings which demonstrate that paid apps have a higher rating on average to free apps! The difference is almost negligible at a mean of 4.18 for free apps and 4.26 for paid apps, but there does exist a higher sense of app satisfaction with paying directly for an app.

Leading on to the third analysis of app categories and their ratings suggests that for developers looking to create apps in less crowded (and higher rated) categories could potentially go down the avenue of creating apps for the events, education and art and design categories as these only account for ~2.5% of this dataset while having the highest average rating.

While it also showed that dating, tools, and maps and navigation apps should be avoided as this area is more crowded and lower rated within this sample.

 

So the question is: How will YOU differentiate yourself in the Google Play Store?
